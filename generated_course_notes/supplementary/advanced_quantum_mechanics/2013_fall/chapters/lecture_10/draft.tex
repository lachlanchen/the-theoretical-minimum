\chapter{Fermions, Dirac Matrices, and the Dirac Sea}

These notes follow Leonard Susskind's Advanced Quantum Mechanics Lecture 10, in which the advertised destination is the Dirac sea but the route begins elsewhere. Susskind first returns to fermionic second quantization, then reviews the one-dimensional Dirac construction, then rebuilds the three-dimensional massless equation before showing why mass forces a larger matrix algebra. Only after that preparation does he come back to negative energy, holes, and positrons. The present chapter is written as a polished companion to that lecture, with explicit credit to Leonard Susskind and curation by LazyingArt LLC. We use units \(c=\hbar=1\).

\section{Fermionic Exchange and Anti-Commutation}

Susskind begins by stepping back from the three-dimensional Dirac equation and asking a simpler question: how do bosons and fermions differ when we build many-particle states from creation and annihilation operators?

For a two-particle state we may write
\begin{equation}
|x,y\rangle=\psi^\dagger(x)\psi^\dagger(y)|0\rangle,
\end{equation}
where \(x\) and \(y\) label the positions of particle \(1\) and particle \(2\). For bosons the exchanged state is the same state,
\begin{equation}
|x,y\rangle=|y,x\rangle,
\end{equation}
and this is implemented by commuting creation operators,
\begin{equation}
\psi^\dagger(x)\psi^\dagger(y)=\psi^\dagger(y)\psi^\dagger(x).
\end{equation}

For fermions the exchanged state differs by a minus sign,
\begin{equation}
|x,y\rangle=-|y,x\rangle.
\end{equation}
Translating this into operator language gives the central algebraic step:
\begin{equation}
\psi^\dagger(x)\psi^\dagger(y)=-\,\psi^\dagger(y)\psi^\dagger(x).
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}
For two operators \(A\) and \(B\), the anti-commutator is
\begin{equation}
\{A,B\}=AB+BA.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Thus the fermionic creation operators satisfy
\begin{equation}
\{\psi^\dagger(x),\psi^\dagger(y)\}=0.
\end{equation}
This is the lecture's first major mathematical move: exchange antisymmetry becomes operator anti-commutation.

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.76\textwidth]{lecture_10_figure_02.png}
\caption{Fermionic minus-sign operator step from exchange antisymmetry. The board state captures the transition from the exchanged two-particle ket to the anti-commuting creation operators.}
\end{figure}

Susskind immediately specializes to equal points. Setting \(y=x\) in the anti-commutator gives
\begin{align}
\{\psi^\dagger(x),\psi^\dagger(x)\}=0
&\Longrightarrow \psi^\dagger(x)\psi^\dagger(x)+\psi^\dagger(x)\psi^\dagger(x)=0 \\
&\Longrightarrow 2\,\psi^\dagger(x)\psi^\dagger(x)=0 \\
&\Longrightarrow (\psi^\dagger(x))^2=0.
\end{align}
So two identical fermions cannot be created in the same state. In the lecture this is the exclusion principle extracted directly from operator algebra, not introduced as a separate postulate.

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.80\textwidth]{lecture_10_figure_03.png}
\caption{Equal-point anti-commutator and exclusion-principle simplification. The board visibly shows the factor \(2\), the repeated creation-operator product at \(x\), and the vanishing result.}
\end{figure}

\section{Occupation Numbers and Particle-Hole Symmetry}

Susskind does not leave the algebraic discussion immediately. Instead he asks what the operator algebra means for the possible occupation numbers of a single mode.

For bosons the familiar single-mode ladder is
\begin{equation}
[a,a^\dagger]=1,
\qquad
n_{\text{boson}}=0,1,2,\dots
\end{equation}
The ladder continues upward without bound. Creation and annihilation operators are therefore sharply different objects: the annihilation operator eventually hits the bottom and gives zero, while the creation operator never runs out of states above it.

For a single fermionic mode the structure is instead
\begin{equation}
\{a,a^\dagger\}=1,
\qquad
a^2=(a^\dagger)^2=0,
\qquad
n_{\text{fermion}}=0,1.
\end{equation}
There are only two states, empty and filled. The creation operator takes
\[
0 \longrightarrow 1,
\]
but acting once more gives zero because there is no higher state. The annihilation operator takes
\[
1 \longrightarrow 0,
\]
and acting once more also gives zero because there is no lower state.

This is the symmetry Susskind wants to emphasize: for a fermion mode the algebra does not sharply distinguish ``filled'' from ``empty'' in the same way that the bosonic ladder distinguishes ``one more'' from ``one less.'' Physically, creation and annihilation still do different things, but algebraically the two-state system is symmetric enough that later one can reinterpret the annihilation of a negative-energy electron as the creation of a positron. That later move is prepared here.

\section{Review of the One-Dimensional Dirac Construction}

Only after the fermionic algebra has been set up does Susskind say, in effect, let us come back to the Dirac equation. He first reviews the simplest one-dimensional case.

The starting point is
\begin{equation}
i\partial_t\psi=-\,i\partial_x\psi.
\end{equation}
This is a Schr\"odinger equation with Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
H=P,
\end{equation}
so the energy-momentum relation is
\begin{equation}
E=p.
\end{equation}
The particle is massless, and wave packets move rigidly to the right.

That is not yet adequate for a relativistic electron in one dimension, since a massive relativistic particle should satisfy
\begin{equation}
E^2=p^2+m^2.
\end{equation}
The first fix is not mass, but handedness: add a left-moving partner,
\begin{equation}
H\psi_1=P\psi_1,
\qquad
H\psi_2=-P\psi_2.
\end{equation}
Packaging these into a two-component wavefunction
\begin{equation}
\psi=
\begin{pmatrix}
\psi_1\\
\psi_2
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
one introduces a matrix \(\alpha\) with eigenvalues \(+1\) and \(-1\), for example
\begin{equation}
\alpha=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0\\
0&-1
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
so that both equations become
\begin{equation}
H\psi=\alpha P\,\psi.
\end{equation}

This still gives only \(H^2=P^2\). To generate mass Susskind adds a second matrix \(\beta\), which in the lecture is represented by the off-diagonal matrix
\begin{equation}
\beta=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1\\
1&0
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
and writes
\begin{equation}
H=\alpha P+\beta m.
\end{equation}
The required algebra is
\begin{equation}
\alpha^2=1,
\qquad
\beta^2=1,
\qquad
\alpha\beta+\beta\alpha=0.
\end{equation}
Susskind explicitly warns that this anti-commutation is a statement about matrices in the internal two-component space, not about fermionic field operators.

Now the essential derivation is short:
\begin{align}
H^2
&=(\alpha P+\beta m)^2 \\
&=\alpha^2P^2+(\alpha\beta+\beta\alpha)Pm+\beta^2m^2 \\
&=P^2+m^2.
\end{align}
So the mass term works only because \(\beta\) couples the right-moving and left-moving sectors and the cross term vanishes. This is the first appearance of a theme that returns later in a more sophisticated form: a Dirac mass mixes two chiral sectors.

\section{The Three-Dimensional Massless Dirac Equation}

At this point Susskind makes the lecture's clearest transition: how do we do this in three dimensions?

The one-dimensional Hamiltonian \(H=\alpha P\) cannot simply be copied, because in three space dimensions the momentum is a vector. If the Hamiltonian is to be rotationally sensible and remain linear in momentum, \(\alpha\) must also become a vector of matrices:
\begin{equation}
H=\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot\mathbf p
=\alpha_x p_x+\alpha_y p_y+\alpha_z p_z.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{lecture_10_figure_04.png}
\caption{Three-dimensional Dirac ansatz \(H=\boldsymbol{\alpha}\cdot\mathbf p\). The board shows the fresh start into the three-dimensional theory and the component expansion across the top line.}
\end{figure}

For a massless relativistic particle we want
\begin{equation}
E^2=\mathbf p^2=p_x^2+p_y^2+p_z^2.
\end{equation}
Squaring the Hamiltonian gives
\begin{align}
H^2
&=\alpha_x^2p_x^2+\alpha_y^2p_y^2+\alpha_z^2p_z^2 \\
&\quad +(\alpha_x\alpha_y+\alpha_y\alpha_x)p_xp_y \\
&\quad +(\alpha_x\alpha_z+\alpha_z\alpha_x)p_xp_z \\
&\quad +(\alpha_y\alpha_z+\alpha_z\alpha_y)p_yp_z.
\end{align}
Therefore we must require
\begin{equation}
\alpha_x^2=\alpha_y^2=\alpha_z^2=1,
\qquad
\alpha_i\alpha_j+\alpha_j\alpha_i=0
\quad (i\neq j).
\end{equation}

The lecture's answer is immediate: the Pauli matrices satisfy exactly this algebra,
\begin{equation}
\sigma_x=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1\\
1&0
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\sigma_y=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&-i\\
i&0
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\sigma_z=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&0\\
0&-1
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Thus one may choose
\begin{equation}
\alpha_x=\sigma_x,
\qquad
\alpha_y=\sigma_y,
\qquad
\alpha_z=\sigma_z,
\end{equation}
so that the Hamiltonian becomes
\begin{equation}
H=\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\mathbf p.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.76\textwidth]{lecture_10_figure_05.png}
\caption{Massless Hamiltonian summarized as \(H=\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\mathbf p\). The lower board compresses the full construction into one line, while the upper board still preserves the final Pauli identification.}
\end{figure}

A clean mathematical way to phrase Susskind's physical interpretation is
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\mathbf p
=
|\mathbf p|\,(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\hat{\mathbf p}),
\qquad
\hat{\mathbf p}=\frac{\mathbf p}{|\mathbf p|}.
\end{equation}
Since \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\hat{\mathbf p}\) has eigenvalues \(\pm1\), the energy eigenvalues are
\begin{equation}
E=\pm |\mathbf p|.
\end{equation}
Positive energy corresponds to spin aligned with the momentum; negative energy corresponds to spin anti-aligned with the momentum.

\begin{remark}
The transcript briefly corrupts the discussion at the point where Susskind explains that spin does not ``drop out'' of the Dirac equation. The intended logic is nevertheless clear. Dirac wanted a Hamiltonian linear in momentum and found that the required coefficients could not be ordinary numbers: they had to be Pauli matrices. In that sense spin is not inserted by hand after the fact; it appears as a consequence of the linearization program.
\end{remark}

\section{Why Two Components Are Not Enough For Mass}

Having found the massless three-dimensional Hamiltonian, Susskind immediately tests the obvious next move:
\begin{equation}
H=\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\mathbf p+\beta m.
\end{equation}
If \(\beta^2=1\), then squaring \(H\) produces the desired \(m^2\) term, but only if the cross term vanishes. That requires
\begin{equation}
\sigma_i\beta+\beta\sigma_i=0
\qquad
\text{for } i=x,y,z.
\end{equation}
In other words, \(\beta\) would have to anti-commute with all three Pauli matrices.

This is precisely what cannot happen in \(2\times2\) matrices. The Pauli matrices already exhaust the available family of mutually anti-commuting \(2\times2\) matrices. So the two-component theory is intrinsically massless in three dimensions.

The lecture's next move is then forced rather than arbitrary: enlarge the internal space and search for \(4\times4\) matrices. One clean representation, consistent with Susskind's block description, is
\begin{equation}
\alpha_i=
\begin{pmatrix}
\sigma_i&0\\
0&-\sigma_i
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\beta=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&I\\
I&0
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
\{\alpha_i,\alpha_j\}=2\delta_{ij},
\qquad
\{\alpha_i,\beta\}=0,
\qquad
\beta^2=1.
\end{equation}
The Hamiltonian then takes the block form
\begin{equation}
H=
\begin{pmatrix}
\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\mathbf P & mI\\
mI & -\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\mathbf P
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
and its square is
\begin{equation}
H^2=\mathbf P^2+m^2.
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
The lecture emphasizes that many equivalent representations of the Dirac matrices exist. The specific \(4\times4\) matrices written here should be understood as a standard reconstruction matching Susskind's block-wise description, not as the only possible choice.
\end{remark}

What matters physically is the algebra, not the particular arrangement of blocks. The mathematical obstruction in \(2\times2\) form is what motivates the enlarged spinor space.

\section{Chirality, Velocity, and Spin}

Susskind next turns from formal construction to interpretation.

\paragraph{Chirality.}
In the massless \(2\times2\) equation, positive energy forces the spin to align in a definite way relative to the momentum. Susskind interprets this as a handedness. When the \(4\times4\) theory is introduced, the doubled spinor contains both handed sectors, and the off-diagonal mass term mixes them. In this sense the mass term couples left-handed and right-handed sectors. For a massless particle, chirality is conserved; with mass, transitions between the two sectors become possible.

\paragraph{Velocity.}
The lecture's most striking interpretation concerns the matrices \(\alpha_i\). Susskind asks what physical observable they represent and answers: velocity.

He derives this from the Heisenberg equation of motion. Using the standard convention
\begin{equation}
\dot L=i[H,L],
\end{equation}
and the canonical commutator
\begin{equation}
[x,P_x]=i,
\end{equation}
we find
\begin{align}
\dot x
&=i[H,x] \\
&=i[\alpha_xP_x+\alpha_yP_y+\alpha_zP_z+\beta m,\;x] \\
&=i\alpha_x[P_x,x] \\
&=\alpha_x.
\end{align}
Thus the \(x\)-component of velocity is \(\alpha_x\), and similarly for the other components.

\begin{remark}
Susskind explicitly says in the lecture that he does not remember the sign convention in the Heisenberg equation. The convention used here is the standard one, and it reproduces the lecture's physical conclusion \(\dot x=\alpha_x\).
\end{remark}

Since each \(\alpha_i\) has eigenvalues \(\pm1\), an instantaneous measurement of a velocity component gives only \(\pm1\) in these units. But \(\alpha_x\) is not conserved:
\begin{equation}
\dot\alpha_x=i[H,\alpha_x]\neq0.
\end{equation}
The Hamiltonian contains matrices that do not commute with \(\alpha_x\), so the velocity operator oscillates in time. This is Susskind's discussion of \emph{zitterbewegung}: a rapid trembling motion whose time average reproduces the familiar smooth relation between momentum and velocity in the slow-particle limit.

\paragraph{Spin.}
The lecture then draws an important distinction. The matrices \(\alpha_i\) are not the genuine spin operator. They behave as an ordinary vector, whereas spin is an axial vector. In the block representation above, a natural spin operator is
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_i=
\begin{pmatrix}
\sigma_i&0\\
0&\sigma_i
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
which differs from \(\alpha_i\) by the sign in the lower block. So the Dirac electron carries two kinds of binary structure in Susskind's discussion: one related to chirality and one related to actual spin. His quaternion digression points toward the special algebraic role of the Pauli matrices, but it does not affect the later logic and can be left in the background.

\section{Negative Energy, Dirac Sea, and Positrons}

Only now does Susskind return to the topic promised at the beginning. He simplifies the discussion as much as possible: the full three-dimensional Dirac equation is not needed yet. The problem is already visible in the one-dimensional right-moving massless case,
\begin{equation}
H=P.
\end{equation}
Because \(P\) can be negative, the theory contains negative-energy states.

This is dangerous. The vacuum should be the state of lowest energy, but if arbitrarily many negative-energy particles could be added, the energy could be driven downward without bound. The crucial point is that the particles under discussion are fermions. Because only one fermion can occupy a given state, the negative-energy states can be filled only once.

This leads to Dirac's historical proposal: define the vacuum to be the state in which all negative-energy fermion states are occupied. Then there is no room for any further negative-energy electron, and the vacuum is stabilized. Susskind stresses that this is a historically powerful picture even if modern language phrases the same mathematics differently.

The consequence is immediate. There are now two kinds of excitations above the filled sea.

First, one may add an ordinary positive-energy electron. Second, one may remove a negative-energy electron from the filled sea. The missing electron leaves behind a hole. Since the removed object carried negative charge, the hole carries positive charge. Since removing a negative-energy particle increases the energy, the hole has positive energy. That hole is the positron.

The lecture then revisits the earlier algebraic symmetry of fermionic creation and annihilation operators. In the negative-energy sector, the annihilation of an electron can be relabeled as the creation of a positron. Schematically one writes
\begin{equation}
b^\dagger(p)\equiv a(-p),
\end{equation}
meaning that the operator which would have annihilated a negative-energy electron is reinterpreted as creating a positive-energy positron.

With this relabeling, the fermion field splits into two pieces,
\begin{equation}
\psi(x)\sim \int_0^\infty dp\, a(p)e^{-ipx}
+
\int_0^\infty dp\, b^\dagger(p)e^{ipx}.
\end{equation}
The exact phase conventions and normalizations are not the point here; Susskind is emphasizing the structure. One part annihilates positive-energy electrons, and the other creates positrons.

Now insert this into the schematic interaction term
\begin{equation}
H_{\mathrm{int}}\sim \psi^\dagger(x)\psi(x)A(x).
\end{equation}
The same operator structure contains several processes at once: an electron can emit or absorb a photon; an electron and a positron can annihilate into a photon; and a photon can create an electron-positron pair. Susskind presents this as an early glimpse of the many channels later organized systematically by Feynman diagrams.

The lecture closes by translating the historical Dirac-sea picture into a more modern language. Instead of talking about a vacuum literally filled with negative-energy electrons, one simply flips the roles of operators in the negative-energy sector and rewrites the theory directly in terms of positive-energy electrons and positive-energy positrons. In that form the symmetry between the two becomes manifest.

He then gives the solid-state analogy. In a crystal, electrons fill the available states up to the Fermi level; removing one electron from the filled sea leaves a hole, and that hole behaves as a positive charge carrier. The pattern is the same: a filled sea, excitations above it, and holes within it. Susskind notes that Dirac's reasoning came first historically, even though condensed-matter language now makes the analogy feel natural.

Finally, when asked about bosons, he is careful not to force the same narrative onto them. The Klein-Gordon theory has a different structure; it is not of the simple linear Dirac form \(H\propto P\), and Susskind does not pursue it here. The point of the lecture is narrower: the Dirac negative-energy problem is stabilized precisely because the particles are fermions.

\section{Summary}

The lecture's architecture is deliberate. Fermionic anti-commutation comes first, because without exclusion and the two-state occupation structure the later hole interpretation would seem unprepared. The one-dimensional Dirac review comes next, because the three-dimensional equation is presented as its forced generalization. The massless three-dimensional Hamiltonian \(H=\boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\mathbf p\) then shows how spin emerges from the demand of linearity in momentum, while the failure of a \(2\times2\) mass term forces the enlargement to \(4\times4\) Dirac matrices and makes chirality mixing possible.

Only after these algebraic and kinematical steps are in place does Susskind return to negative energy. The filled Dirac sea, the positron as a hole, the decomposition of the field operator into electron and positron pieces, and the multi-channel meaning of \(\psi^\dagger\psi A\) all rely on the fermionic structure introduced at the beginning. In that sense the lecture is not a loose collection of topics: its final physical payoff depends on the exact order in which the mathematics was built.
