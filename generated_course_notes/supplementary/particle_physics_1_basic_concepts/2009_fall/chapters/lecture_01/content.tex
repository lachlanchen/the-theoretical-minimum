\chapter{New Revolutions in Particle Physics: Basic Concepts}

These companion notes to Leonard Susskind's first lecture in \emph{Particle Physics 1: Basic Concepts}, curated by LazyingArt LLC, follow the lecture's deliberate order of motivation: first the oldest question, then the first evidence, then the first true experiments, and only after that the equations that begin to control the subject. We will therefore let the chapter unfold as the lecture unfolds. The point is not simply to list facts about particles, but to see how chemistry, radioactivity, wave optics, quantum mechanics, and relativity lock together into the working logic of particle physics.

\section{Discrete Matter Or Continuous Fields}

Particle physics begins here with an ancient question: is nature discrete, or is it continuous? The atomist answer says that matter is built from indivisible units. Susskind immediately modernizes the vocabulary: let us call such units particles. The opposite answer is that matter is not built from separate pieces at all, but is instead spread out continuously through space, a kind of smear.

This is why the lecture starts by opposing particle language to field language. A field is something defined throughout space: a density field, an electric field, a magnetic field. Such quantities are not pictured as little pellets sitting at isolated points. They are extended, continuous descriptions. Particle language comes with words such as atoms, molecules, collisions, and beams. Continuum language comes with words such as density, field, and smooth distribution.

The first surprise of the lecture is that quantum mechanics will eventually prevent either side from winning outright. The discrete picture is not simply right, and the continuum picture is not simply wrong. In some sense, each is incomplete; in some sense, each survives.

\begin{remark}
The lecture explicitly warns that the history is being organized logically rather than strictly chronologically. We should preserve that warning. The aim is not historical pedantry but conceptual sequence.
\end{remark}

There is also a pedagogical warning embedded in the opening. We are not going to rederive all of quantum mechanics, relativity, and electromagnetism from scratch. The lecture assumes enough of those subjects to move quickly, but it still tries to stage the basic facts in a self-contained way.

\section{Chemistry, Atoms, And The First Evidence For Discreteness}

The first persuasive evidence for discreteness is presented as a chemical fact. Dalton compares equal numbers of molecules of different substances and finds that their masses are close to integer multiples of the hydrogen case. The hidden story about how one compares equal numbers of molecules is acknowledged in the lecture and then put aside; the point that matters for the present argument is the mass pattern itself.

In cleaned notation, the content may be summarized as
\begin{equation}
M_{\text{mole of }X} \approx n_X\, M_{\text{mole of hydrogen}},
\qquad n_X \in \mathbb{Z}.
\end{equation}

The lecture insists on the character of this statement. The multiples are not arbitrary. They are not \(3/2\), not \(\pi\), not some irregular decimal accident. They are close to integers. This is what suggests building blocks.

Susskind then immediately refuses suspense and translates Dalton into modern language. Ordinary matter is made of atoms, and atomic masses are dominated by protons and neutrons. Electrons matter chemically and electrically, but in the mass budget they are tiny corrections. At the level needed here we may write
\begin{equation}
M_{\text{atom}} \approx N_p m_p + N_n m_n,
\end{equation}
with \(m_p \approx m_n\), and with the electron contribution suppressed because it is so small.

Hydrogen is special because its nucleus is just one proton. That is why hydrogen naturally serves as the unit against which Dalton's integer pattern is read. The lecture's point is not that Dalton had our modern nuclear model, but that his chemistry was already pointing toward matter as something assembled from discrete ingredients.

At the end of this story, nineteenth-century chemistry had apparently finished the job. The elements had been identified, the periodic table had been organized, and atoms themselves looked like the elementary particles of nature. This is exactly why the next historical turn matters. If atoms had really been the end of the story, there would have been no particle physics in the modern sense.

\section{Radioactivity As The First Particle-Physics Experiment}

The lecture's next move is not to declare a theory, but to reconstruct an experiment. Radioactivity first appears as something that mysteriously ruins a photographic plate. A radioactive material placed nearby acts \emph{as if} it were emitting some kind of radiation. Becquerel then improves the setup. Put the material deep inside a lead block, give the radiation a narrow opening through which to escape, and place a plate at some distance. What appears is a sharp spot. Something is coming out in a collimated beam.

That already has the shape of particle physics: a source, a collimator, a beam, and a detector.

Now the magnetic field is introduced. The single beam splits into three. One goes straight ahead, behaving as if it is neutral. One bends one way, behaving as if negatively charged. One bends the other way, behaving as if positively charged. The field does not merely perturb the beam; it classifies it.

At this point the lecture again removes suspense. The three components are
\begin{itemize}
\item \(\beta\) radiation: electrons;
\item \(\alpha\) radiation: helium nuclei, made of two protons and two neutrons;
\item \(\gamma\) radiation: photons.
\end{itemize}

The order of explanation matters. First comes the apparatus, then the beam, then the magnetic splitting, then the naming. The experiment teaches us what sort of distinctions nature is making before we are given the final identifications.

\subsection{Question \& Answer}

\paragraph{Question.} Why does Susskind call this, in some sense, the first particle-physics experiment?

\paragraph{Answer.} Because the setup already contains the essential grammar of the subject. There is a source, a collimated beam, a field that probes the beam, and a detector that records separated outcomes. Moreover, if the source is sufficiently dilute, the detector would not simply glow; it would click or blotch one event at a time. The language of particles enters here through discrete detection.

This last point is crucial in the lecture. One can imagine the plate acting like a counter. Instead of a continuous patch one would see blip, blip, blip. The radiation would then reveal itself not merely as a beam, but as something arriving in discrete little bundles.

The \(\gamma\) component is the bridge to the rest of the lecture. It is neutral, it arrives in discrete events, and eventually we will identify it with light. That identification does not change the subject; it forces the lecture to reopen the entire wave story under more severe pressure.

\section{Why Light First Looked Like A Wave}

Once \(\gamma\)-radiation has been identified with high-energy light, the lecture deliberately steps backward. Instead of rushing to photons, it rebuilds the classical case for light as a wave. This is one of the lecture's most important transitions: \(\gamma\)-rays are not a separate topic from light. They are the high-energy end of the same electromagnetic story.

Classically, light is the strongest case for continuity. It is not first presented as a stream of bullets, but as a propagating disturbance of the electromagnetic field. When charges accelerate, electromagnetic waves are emitted. Maxwell's achievement is then to say what these waves are: an oscillating electric field together with an oscillating magnetic field, each perpendicular to the other, propagating through space.

But the lecture does not start with Maxwell's equations. It starts with experiments that force the wave idea.

\subsection{Question \& Answer}

\paragraph{Question.} Does the spreading of light through a single aperture already prove that light is a wave?

\paragraph{Answer.} No. This is a local conceptual obstacle that Susskind takes seriously. A beam passing through one hole produces a fuzzy spot rather than a perfectly sharp geometric image, but that alone is not decisive. One might still imagine particles being deflected by the edges of the hole. The single-aperture experiment suggests wave behavior, but it does not yet compel it.

What compels it is the two-slit experiment. If light were only particles, we would expect two overlapping smears on the screen. Instead we find bright and dark bands: places where the contributions reinforce, and places where they cancel. This is the experimental signature of interference.

After that, Maxwell enters naturally. Light is a wave of electric and magnetic field, and because it is a wave, it exhibits interference.

\section{Wave Kinematics: Wavelength, Period, Frequency, And Angular Frequency}

Only after the wave picture has been firmly established does the lecture slow down and define the variables that describe wave motion. This delay is pedagogically important. The equations are not dropped into empty space; they answer questions created by the preceding discussion.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{lecture_01_figure_02.png}
\caption{Introducing \(\lambda\) as wavelength. The board shows only the beginning of the word after the equals sign; the lecture makes the intended reading explicit.}
\end{figure}

The first variable is the wavelength \(\lambda\), the spatial length of one complete cycle. In the lecture the notation is introduced directly:
\begin{equation}
\lambda = \text{wavelength}.
\end{equation}

The second variable is the period \(T\), the time required for one full cycle to pass a fixed observer. The lecture emphasizes the contrast: wavelength is a distance; period is a time.

\subsection{Question \& Answer}

\paragraph{Question.} What is the connection between wavelength and period?

\paragraph{Answer.} This is exactly the way the lecture motivates the relation. In one period the wave advances by one wavelength. Therefore
\begin{equation}
v = \frac{\lambda}{T},
\end{equation}
where \(v\) is the speed of the wave. For light, \(v=c\). For water waves or sound waves, the same formula holds with a different speed.

It is then more convenient to use frequency rather than period. By definition,
\begin{equation}
f=\frac{1}{T}.
\end{equation}

Substituting this into the previous relation gives
\begin{equation}
\lambda f = v.
\end{equation}
For light we therefore obtain
\begin{equation}
\lambda f = c.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{lecture_01_figure_03.png}
\caption{Frequency and wavelength relations on the board. The lecture has already introduced the period, and here the board records the compact relations \(f=\frac{1}{T}\) and \(\lambda f=c\).}
\end{figure}

This is the first place where the lecture's mathematics becomes a genuine spine. Long wavelength means low frequency; short wavelength means high frequency. The equation makes this precise:
\begin{equation}
f=\frac{c}{\lambda}.
\end{equation}

Now comes another notational shift. Physicists prefer not to speak in cycles per second when writing formulas. They use angular frequency \(\omega\), measured in radians per second. The relation is
\begin{equation}
\omega = 2\pi f.
\end{equation}

Substituting \(f=\omega/(2\pi)\) into \(f=c/\lambda\), we get
\begin{align}
\frac{\omega}{2\pi} &= \frac{c}{\lambda}, \\
\omega &= \frac{2\pi c}{\lambda}.
\end{align}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
\omega\lambda = 2\pi c.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{lecture_01_figure_04.png}
\caption{Fundamental wave-motion relations on the board. The upper conversion between \(f\) and \(\omega\) is only partly crisp in the frame, but the boxed lower relation \(\omega=\frac{2\pi c}{\lambda}\) is clear and is the lecture's emphasized takeaway.}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Question \& Answer}

\paragraph{Question.} Why does the lecture insist on \(\omega\) when \(f\) already seems simpler?

\paragraph{Answer.} Susskind actually pauses over this question. The answer is not metaphysical. One writes formulas in both conventions and asks which version accumulates fewer stray factors of \(2\pi\). The lecture's answer is that the \(\omega\)-convention is typically cleaner in actual physics. Thus the notation shift is not cosmetic; it is meant to make later formulas more natural.

At this stage the lecture has not yet said anything quantum mechanical. We have only the kinematics of waves. But this is the exact set of variables needed for the next transition.

\section{Photons And The Wave-Particle Puzzle}

We now return to light, but not yet to all of quantum mechanics. The lecture reopens the question experimentally. Imagine shining light on a screen. At ordinary intensity we see a continuous illuminated patch, perhaps a blob, perhaps an interference pattern. Now place a semi-opaque filter in the beam and keep dimming it. At first the blob merely becomes dimmer. But eventually the character of the result changes. One no longer sees a continuous patch. One sees isolated events: discrete blips.

This is not yet the whole story. If one waits long enough, the same blips accumulate into the same pattern that the bright beam had produced. If the bright beam gave a smooth interference pattern, the attenuated beam gives one-at-a-time events that slowly fill in that same interference pattern.

This is the lecture's first real quantum paradox. Light was already established as a wave. Yet under attenuation it arrives in discrete acts of detection.

\subsection{Question \& Answer}

\paragraph{Question.} What, then, survives from the wave description once the detections become discrete?

\paragraph{Answer.} The wave survives as a law of probabilities. The lecture states the point plainly: the individual hits are random, but the probability of a hit at a given location is governed by the same wave pattern that classical optics had already established. In that sense the wave theory becomes a theory of probabilities for particle-like events.

This is why the lecture refuses to say simply that light is a wave or simply that light is a particle. It has manifestations of both. And the same is true more generally. Electrons, though obviously particles, form interference patterns when sent one at a time through a two-slit arrangement. Even much larger composite systems, such as buckyballs, do so as well. The lecture's point is therefore not that light is exceptional, but that wave-particle duality is general.

Now we are ready for the first quantum equation of the lecture. A photon of angular frequency \(\omega\) carries energy
\begin{equation}
E_\gamma = \hbar\omega.
\end{equation}

This formula is immediately linked back to the wave kinematics. Since \(\omega=2\pi c/\lambda\), short wavelength means large \(\omega\), and therefore large energy per photon. The lecture insists on exactly this moral: a single short-wavelength photon carries much more energy than a single long-wavelength photon.

At the same time, a low-frequency beam may still have a large total energy if it contains many photons. If a pulse contains \(N\) photons of the same frequency, then
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{beam}} = N\hbar\omega.
\end{equation}

This is a point that the lecture motivates verbally and that the notes should keep visible. A radio beam can carry a huge total energy, but that does not mean an individual radio photon is energetic. It means there are many photons. The discreteness remains: the total energy comes in integer multiples of \(\hbar\omega\).

We are now standing at the point where the lecture explicitly says that these equations will explain why particle physics needs large machines. But before that payoff can be extracted, one more reset is needed.

\section{Relativity, Natural Units, And Why High Energy Sees Small Distance}

The lecture now says, in effect: we have been through waves and particles; let us come to another set of concepts. Particle physics is relativistic quantum mechanics. That is why the next move is not optional. We must bring in relativity.

The famous formula is
\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{rest}} = mc^2.
\end{equation}
But the lecture is very careful about what this means in modern language. Mass is not a velocity-dependent quantity. What older books called \emph{rest mass} is what modern particle physics simply calls mass. Thus \(m\) is an invariant property of the particle, while energy depends on the frame.

\subsection{Question \& Answer}

\paragraph{Question.} What, exactly, is being asserted when we write \(E=mc^2\)?

\paragraph{Answer.} In the lecture's modern usage, the equation refers to the energy of a massive object in a frame where that object has no net motion. That is why we have written \(E_{\mathrm{rest}}\). If the object is moving relative to us, its energy is different. The mass is the same; the energy is not.

The lecture gives two examples. First, take a box of gas at rest. Heat it. The molecules inside move more violently, so the box has more internal energy even though the box as a whole remains at rest. Therefore its rest mass increases. Second, bring together an electron and a positron at rest. Their rest energy does not disappear; it reappears as photon energy. In schematic form,
\begin{equation}
2m_e c^2 = E_{\gamma,1}+E_{\gamma,2}.
\end{equation}

The conceptual lesson is that mass and energy, for a system at rest, are two unit systems attached to the same physical content.

This is where the lecture pauses to discuss numerical values of \(c\) and \(\hbar\). The transcript becomes garbled in the details, but the main point is clear and worth preserving: the decimal values themselves are not sacred. They depend on the chosen units. That is exactly why natural units are useful.

\subsection{Question \& Answer}

\paragraph{Question.} Why do particle physicists so often set \(c=\hbar=1\)?

\paragraph{Answer.} Because these are not merely convenient constants; they are universal ones. The speed of light constrains all motion. Planck's constant sets the quantum scale for all systems. They are not like the mass of the proton or the size of a particular atom, which are special only to particular objects. Since \(c\) and \(\hbar\) are universal, it is natural to absorb them into our units.

Thus we often choose
\begin{equation}
c=1, \qquad \hbar=1.
\end{equation}

The lecture also explains why one does \emph{not} usually set \(G=1\) in ordinary particle-physics notes. One could, and in gravitation or quantum gravity one often does. But ordinary particle physics is overwhelmingly insensitive to gravity. The useful universal constants for this subject are \(c\) and \(\hbar\).

Once \(c=1\), the rest-energy relation simplifies to
\begin{equation}
E=m.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{lecture_01_figure_05.png}
\caption{Rest-energy relation in natural units. The lecture is stressing the simplification \(E=m\) after choosing \(c=1\). The lower line on the board is not sufficiently legible to be promoted here beyond a dimensional mnemonic.}
\end{figure}

Likewise, once \(\hbar=1\), the photon-energy formula becomes
\begin{equation}
E_\gamma=\omega.
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
These equations must not be flattened into one another. \(E_{\mathrm{rest}}=mc^2\) is a statement about a massive object at rest. \(E=m\) is the same statement only after setting \(c=1\). \(E_\gamma=\hbar\omega\), or \(E_\gamma=\omega\) after setting \(\hbar=1\), is the photon-energy formula. A photon has energy but no rest mass.
\end{remark}

The lecture then turns from energy to momentum. For nonrelativistic motion we have
\begin{equation}
\vec p = m\vec v.
\end{equation}
That formula is familiar, but it is not the right one to use for light. A light beam has no rest mass, yet it does carry momentum. The lecture states the correct relation directly:
\begin{equation}
p=\frac{E}{c},
\qquad\text{or equivalently}\qquad
E=cp,
\end{equation}
for a collimated beam of light.

Here again the lecture passes through a short conceptual obstacle before moving on. The formula looks odd at first sight because momentum used to have a velocity upstairs and now seems to have one downstairs. The resolution is dimensional: energy has units of mass times velocity squared, so \(E/c\) has the same units as momentum.

\paragraph{Worked derivation of photon momentum.}
Now the lecture turns this into a short exercise. For one photon,
\begin{align}
p_\gamma &= \frac{E_\gamma}{c} \\
&= \frac{\hbar\omega}{c}.
\end{align}
Using the wave relation
\begin{equation}
\omega = \frac{2\pi c}{\lambda},
\end{equation}
we find
\begin{align}
p_\gamma
&= \frac{\hbar}{c}\,\frac{2\pi c}{\lambda} \\
&= \frac{2\pi\hbar}{\lambda} \\
&= \frac{h}{\lambda}.
\end{align}

The lecture's spoken algebra gets momentarily tangled over where the \(2\pi\) sits, but the endpoint is standard and unambiguous:
\begin{equation}
p_\gamma = \frac{h}{\lambda}.
\end{equation}

This is the final mathematical bridge of the lecture. Shorter wavelength means larger momentum. Since shorter wavelength also means larger \(\omega\), it means larger energy as well. The microscope argument then becomes unavoidable. To resolve a small object, one must use radiation with wavelength comparable to or smaller than the object. That in turn requires high momentum and high energy.

The lecture makes this vivid before making it general. Radio waves cannot resolve facial features because their wavelengths are too long. To see a hair, one needs wavelength on the scale of the hair's thickness. To see atomic structure, one needs wavelength on the scale of atomic size. Thus
\begin{equation}
\text{smaller target scale}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\text{shorter wavelength}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\text{larger momentum and energy}.
\end{equation}

This is the operational heart of particle physics. We build bigger accelerators because higher-energy particles have shorter wavelengths, and shorter wavelengths let us probe smaller structures. The lecture ends by observing that if there is a fundamental lower limit to this progression, it is plausibly near the Planck length; but that scale is still far beyond present machines.

\section{Summary}

The lecture begins with a philosophical contrast and ends with an experimental program. Matter may be described in particle language or field language, but quantum mechanics forces both descriptions to survive in altered form. Dalton's chemistry supplies the first strong evidence for discreteness. Radioactivity supplies the first true particle-physics apparatus: source, beam, field, detector. Light then reopens the continuum side of the story, and wave optics forces us through diffraction, interference, and Maxwell's electromagnetic waves before quantum mechanics turns the wave pattern into a probability law for discrete detections.

Only after that preparation do the central formulas acquire their full weight:
\[
\lambda f=c, \qquad \omega=\frac{2\pi c}{\lambda}, \qquad E_\gamma=\hbar\omega,
\qquad E_{\mathrm{rest}}=mc^2, \qquad p_\gamma=\frac{h}{\lambda}.
\]
Taken together, they explain the practical logic of the field. To see smaller things we need shorter wavelengths; shorter wavelengths mean larger momentum and energy; and larger energies demand larger machines. That is the lecture's first great unifying theme, and it is the theme on which the rest of the course is built.
