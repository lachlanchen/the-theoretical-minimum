## Definitions And Objects

- \(M\): magnetization field/order parameter for the ferromagnet; nonzero \(M\) signals a chosen direction in the vacuum.
- Goldstone boson: low-energy excitation produced by slowly varying the broken-symmetry direction in space; in the ferromagnet these are magnons/spin waves.
- Vacuum: minimum of the ordinary potential energy; in the field-theory part, “where the theory likes to sit.”
- Chiral superfield \(\Phi\): complex superfield depending effectively on \(y\) and \(\theta\), not taken to be real.
- Components of \(\Phi\): scalar \(\phi\), fermion \(\psi\), auxiliary field \(F\).
- Auxiliary field \(F\): no kinetic term; enters algebraically and is eliminated by its equation of motion.
- Superpotential: lecture writes a Roman-looking \(v\); cleaned bank should identify it with \(W(\Phi)\).
- Ordinary scalar potential: the non-superspace potential energy of the scalar field, derived after eliminating \(F\).
- Hidden sector: weakly coupled sector that breaks supersymmetry while the visible sector only sees small splittings.
- Goldstino: massless fermion produced when supersymmetry acts on a non-supersymmetric vacuum.
- \(SU(5)\) generators: traceless Hermitian \(5\times 5\) matrices.
- \(SU(2)\) inside \(SU(5)\): upper-left \(2\times 2\) block generated by \(\tau_1,\tau_2,\tau_3\).
- \(SU(3)\) inside \(SU(5)\): lower-right \(3\times 3\) block generated by the eight \(\lambda\)-matrices.
- \(Y\): diagonal generator commuting with both block subalgebras; distinguishes the \(2\)-block from the \(3\)-block.
- In the \(SU(5)\) discussion, fermions are listed as left-handed particles or left-handed antiparticles.

## Derivation Steps

Free chiral action from \(\int d^4\theta\,\Phi^\dagger\Phi\)

1. Expand \(\Phi=\phi+\psi\theta+F\theta^2\) and \(\Phi^\dagger\).
2. Multiply \(\Phi^\dagger\Phi\).
3. Interpret \(d^4\theta\) as “pick out the \(\theta^2\bar\theta^2\) term.”
4. Read off the scalar kinetic term.
5. Read off the fermion kinetic term \(\bar\psi\sigma^\mu\partial_\mu\psi\).
6. Read off the auxiliary term \(F^\ast F\).

Two-\(\theta\) extraction from \(\Phi^n\)

1. Start from \((\phi+\psi\theta+F\theta^2)^n\).
2. Keep only terms with exactly two \(\theta\)’s, since \(d^2\theta\) kills everything else.
3. One \(F\theta^2\) and \(n-1\) scalar factors gives \(nF\phi^{n-1}\).
4. Two \(\psi\theta\) factors and \(n-2\) scalar factors give \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\phi^{n-2}\psi\psi\).
5. Add the complex conjugate.
6. Recognize \(n\phi^{n-1}=W'(\phi)\) and \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\phi^{n-2}=\frac12 W''(\phi)\).

Eliminating the auxiliary field \(F\)

1. Collect only the \(F\)-dependent terms in the component Lagrangian.
2. Use that \(F\) has no derivatives and no kinetic term.
3. Reduce the Euler-Lagrange equation to \(\partial \mathcal L/\partial F=0\).
4. Solve algebraically for \(F^\ast\) and \(F\).
5. Substitute back into \(F^\ast F\), \(F W'(\phi)\), and \(F^\ast W'(\phi)^\ast\).
6. Combine signs to get the ordinary potential \(V(\phi)=|W'(\phi)|^2\).

Vacuum criterion for supersymmetry breaking

1. Use \(V(\phi)=|W'(\phi)|^2\), hence \(V(\phi)\ge 0\).
2. If the minimum sits at \(V=0\), then \(W'(\phi)=0\).
3. Therefore \(F=0\) in the vacuum.
4. Interpret vanishing \(F\)-component as an unbroken supersymmetric vacuum.
5. If the minimum sits at \(V>0\), then \(W'(\phi)\neq 0\).
6. Therefore \(F\neq 0\) in the vacuum.
7. Interpret nonzero \(F\) as spontaneous supersymmetry breaking and as the source of boson-fermion mass splitting.

Goldstone-to-goldstino pattern

1. In ordinary spontaneous breaking, a non-invariant vacuum means the symmetry generator acting on \(|0\rangle\) gives a new zero-energy state.
2. That new state is the Goldstone mode.
3. In broken supersymmetry, the transformed vacuum must again produce a new zero-energy state.
4. Since supersymmetry changes boson number by one fermion, the new state is fermionic.
5. Therefore broken supersymmetry implies a massless goldstino.

\(SU(n)\) generator counting

1. Parametrize infinitesimal transformations by \(U=1+i\epsilon G\).
2. Unitarity implies \(G\) is Hermitian.
3. The special condition implies \(G\) is traceless.
4. An \(n\times n\) Hermitian matrix carries \(n^2\) real parameters.
5. The trace-zero condition removes one real parameter.
6. Therefore the number of generators is \(n^2-1\).

Embedding \(SU(3)\times SU(2)\times U(1)\) into \(SU(5)\)

1. Split the \(5\times 5\) matrix into a \(2\oplus 3\) block structure.
2. Put the three \(SU(2)\) generators in the upper-left \(2\times 2\) corner.
3. Put the eight \(SU(3)\) generators in the lower-right \(3\times 3\) corner.
4. Add a diagonal generator proportional to the identity on each block so it commutes with both subalgebras.
5. Impose tracelessness to fix the relative diagonal entries.
6. Obtain the \(Y\)-generator with equal entries in the \(2\)-block and equal entries in the \(3\)-block.

Charge-based selection of the five-dimensional multiplet

1. The electric-charge operator must come from a traceless \(SU(5)\) generator.
2. Therefore the charges in one multiplet must sum to zero.
3. The first two slots are naturally \(\nu_e\) and \(e^-\), with charges \(0\) and \(-1\).
4. The remaining three slots must therefore contribute \(+1\) total.
5. Three anti-down quarks contribute \(+\frac13+\frac13+\frac13\).
6. This selects the \((\nu_e,e^-,\bar d,\bar d,\bar d)\) pattern rather than an up-quark choice.

## Notation Choices

- Use \(W(\Phi)\) for the superpotential in the working chapter, even though the lecture writes a Roman-looking \(v\). Reserve \(V(\phi)\) for the ordinary scalar potential.
- Use \(\Phi\) for the chiral superfield and \(\phi\) for its scalar component.
- Use \(\psi\) for the fermion component and \(F\) for the auxiliary component.
- Write \(\theta^2\) as shorthand for \(\theta\theta\).
- Use \(\mathrm{h.c.}\) consistently for the Hermitian conjugate of the chiral \(d^2\theta\) term.
- Preserve the lecture’s non-equality arrow for component extraction: use \(\leadsto\) or equivalent, not \(=\), when showing what survives the \(d^2\theta\) integral.
- In the symmetry-breaking analogy, use \(J\) for the ordinary symmetry generator and \(M\) for magnetization.
- State explicitly once that “vacuum” means the field configuration at the minimum of the ordinary potential.
- In the \(SU(5)\) section, use \(\tau_a\) for the \(SU(2)\) block generators and \(\lambda_A\) for the \(SU(3)\) block generators.
- Use \(Y\) for the diagonal \(U(1)\)-type generator inside \(SU(5)\), but do not fix a normalization beyond the relative entries unless the chapter later needs it.
- In the late group-theory section, keep Susskind’s convention that the fermion lists are written in left-handed language.
- If the chapter later wants representation labels, introduce them cautiously: at the point reached in this lecture, “five-dimensional multiplet” is safer than forcing \(\mathbf{5}\) versus \(\bar{\mathbf{5}}\) too early.
